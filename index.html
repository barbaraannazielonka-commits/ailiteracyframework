AI Literacy Workbook · VG1 · VG2 · VG3

You are not
just a user of AI.

This workbook guides you through four domains of AI literacy from understanding what AI actually is, to using it creatively, managing it critically, and shaping it for the good of society.

Before you begin

How to use this workbook

Each domain contains 4 tasks. You will read, reflect, and actively use AI tools. Your thinking — not the AI's output — is what matters.

🧠

Think first

Before prompting any AI, write your own answer or prediction. Notice what changes afterward.

🤖

Use real AI tools

Each task includes a suggested prompt. Use ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or another tool your teacher approves.

✍️

Reflect in writing

After each task, write in the reflection boxes inside each task. You can type directly into them as you work through the workbook.

⚠️

Be critical

AI can be wrong, biased, or misleading. Part of your job is to catch it — and say so.

Engage with AI

AI is already around you — but where?

Map the AI systems in your daily life before you ever touch a chatbot.

▼
The task

Most AI around you is invisible. It filters your search results, decides what appears in your feed, corrects your spelling, and even influences what music you hear next. Before using any AI tool, let's map the world you're already living in.

  1. Spend 10 minutes listing every digital service or app you used in the last 24 hours (phone, school platforms, streaming, social media, maps, etc.).
  2. For each one, decide: does it use AI? How do you know — or guess?
  3. Mark which ones use predictive AI (recommending, predicting, filtering) and which use generative AI (creating text, images, audio).

Basic to Intermediate

Can you trust this output?

Test an AI's accuracy on something you already know — and learn to fact-check like a professional.

▼
The task

AI-generated content can look totally convincing while being completely wrong. This is called a hallucination — the model produces something statistically plausible, but factually false. Your job is to become a skilled output evaluator.

  1. Choose a topic you genuinely know well — your hometown, a hobby, a sport, a subject you're strong in, or something about Norwegian history or culture.
  2. Ask an AI to explain or describe that topic in a paragraph.
  3. Read the output carefully. Look for errors, oversimplifications, or things that sound right but feel off.
  4. Use a trusted external source (encyclopedia, official site, textbook) to verify the claims.

03
Intermediate

The algorithm and you

Investigate how recommendation systems shape your worldview — and what they're hiding from you.

▼
The task

When YouTube, TikTok, Spotify, or Instagram recommends content to you, a predictive AI is working behind the scenes. It learns from every click, pause, like, and scroll. Over time, this creates a filter bubble — a personalised information environment that can narrow what you see and believe.

  1. Pick one platform you use regularly (YouTube, TikTok, Spotify, Instagram, news apps).
  2. Look at your current recommendations or "for you" feed. What patterns do you notice? What types of content, creators, or viewpoints appear?
  3. Think about what is not appearing — what topics, perspectives, or cultures are absent?
  4. Use an AI tool to help you think through how recommendation algorithms work and what risks they carry.

Who benefits — and who is harmed?

Analyse a real-world AI use case through an ethical lens. Who wins, who loses, and what values are at stake?

▼
The task

AI is not neutral. Every AI system is built by people with specific goals, funded by specific organisations, and trained on data that reflects specific histories and biases. When AI is deployed in society — in schools, hospitals, hiring processes, or policing — its effects are unequal.

Choose one of the following real-world AI use cases:

  • A — AI used by employers to screen job applicants' CVs
  • B — Facial recognition technology used by police
  • C — AI that predicts which students are at risk of dropping out of school
  • D — Social media AI that flags and removes "harmful" content

Your task: use AI to research your chosen case, then write a structured ethical analysis.

Use an AI tool
🤖 Suggested prompt — Research phase
"I am analysing the use of [your chosen AI case] from an ethical perspective. Please give me: (1) a factual explanation of how the AI system works in this context, (2) documented examples of where this AI has caused harm or been found to be biased, (3) who the main beneficiaries are, (4) which groups of people are most likely to be negatively affected, and (5) what regulations or guidelines (if any) currently govern its use in Europe."
🤖 Follow-up prompt — Challenge the AI
"Now steelman the opposing view: give me the strongest argument that this AI system is actually beneficial and that concerns about it are overblown. Then tell me what evidence would be needed to resolve the disagreement."
⚠️ Try to verify at least one claim the AI makes by searching for a news article, report, or academic study. AI can misremember specific cases or statistics.
Your ethical analysis

Write a structured analysis (aim for 200 to 300 words) covering:

  • How the system works and what it is supposed to achieve
  • Who benefits from it and how
  • Who is at risk of harm, and what kinds of harm
  • Whether the benefits justify the risks, and under what conditions
  • Your own position: should this AI be used? With what safeguards?

✦ Domain 2

Create with AI

Use AI as a creative partner — not a ghostwriter. Your ideas come first. AI amplifies, challenges, and extends them. You stay in the driver's seat.

4 Tasks

Collaborative & creative

01
Basic

Your idea first, then the AI's

Write something creative entirely on your own — then see what AI does with the same brief, and reflect on the difference.

▼
The task

The most important rule in creative work with AI: your idea always comes first. If AI goes first, it colonises your imagination. If you go first, AI becomes a mirror that shows you new angles on your own thinking.

  1. Choose a creative brief from the list below (or invent your own).
  2. Write your own response — a paragraph, a few lines of a poem, a scene, a slogan, whatever the format calls for. Spend at least 8 minutes on this. Don't rush.
  3. Then give the same brief to an AI. Do not show it your writing.
  4. Compare the two. What's similar? What's different? What do you like better?

Choose one brief:

  • Write the opening paragraph of a story set in Norway in 2050, after AI has transformed everyday life
  • Write a short poem about something small that matters to you
  • Write a 3-sentence pitch for an app that would genuinely improve your school life
  • Describe a memory using only sensory details (no emotions, no explanations)

02
Intermediate

Iterate, don't accept

Learn to steer AI through multiple rounds of feedback — just like working with any collaborator.

▼
The task

Most people take the first AI output and stop there. That's like accepting the first draft of anything and calling it done. Real creative collaboration — whether with humans or AI — is iterative. You push, redirect, ask for something different, and improve through feedback.

Your mission: create a short informational explainer (about 150 words) about a topic your class is currently studying or that interests you. But you must go through at least 3 rounds of conversation with the AI before you are satisfied.

  1. Give the AI an initial brief.
  2. After each response, write a follow-up that asks for something more specific, different in tone, shorter, clearer, or more relevant to your audience.
  3. Track each round and document what you changed and why.
  4. Write the final version in your own words, using the AI's iterations as raw material.

Intermediate to Advanced

Who owns what we make together?

Explore the thorny questions of originality, authorship, and copyright when AI is involved in creation.

▼
The task

When you use AI to create something — a piece of writing, an image, a piece of music — who is the author? Who owns it? Can you copyright it? Can the AI tool's company use it? Can it be used commercially without crediting the original human artists whose work the AI learned from?

These are not hypothetical questions — they are being argued in courts right now.

  1. Ask an AI to generate something creative in the style of a specific, well-known artist, musician, or author.
  2. Use a separate AI prompt to research the legal and ethical situation around that kind of request.
  3. Form your own view on the question: where do you draw the line?
Your creative experiment

⚖ Domain 3

Manage AI

Know when to use AI, when not to, and how to stay in control. Divide work intelligently between human judgment and machine capability.

4 Tasks

Decision-making & oversight

01
Basic

AI for this — or not?

Build your own framework for deciding when AI is useful, unnecessary, or actually harmful for a task.

▼
The task

Not every task is better with AI. Using AI to write a heartfelt birthday message to your best friend defeats the point. Using AI to translate a document you'd otherwise guess at might be essential. Good AI management starts with knowing the difference.

  1. Look at the list of tasks below. For each one, decide: AI only / AI-supported / Human only / Unsure.
  2. Write a brief reason for each decision.
  3. Then use an AI to check your reasoning — and see whether it agrees or challenges you.
  4. Update your framework based on the conversation.

Task list:

  • Writing a formal complaint letter to your municipality
  • Deciding whether to trust a news article you just read
  • Summarising 20 pages of a textbook before an exam
  • Choosing which university subject to study
  • Translating a document from Norwegian to English
  • Comforting a friend who is going through something hard
  • Preparing for a debate by generating counterarguments
  • Deciding whether a photo of a public figure is real or manipulated

Monitor and course-correct

Use AI for a multi-step research task, but stay in control: catch errors, redirect when needed, and maintain your own oversight throughout.

▼
The task

AI can go off-track. It can drift away from your original goal, start hallucinating, become repetitive, or produce content that sounds right but is misleading. Managing AI means actively monitoring and correcting its course — not just accepting what comes out.

Your task: use AI to help you research a controversial or complex topic for a school subject. The topic must be something where:

  • There are multiple legitimate perspectives
  • Facts matter and can be verified
  • AI might be tempted to oversimplify or take sides

Suggested topics: immigration policy in Norway, the ethics of eating meat, social media and teen mental health, nuclear energy in Europe, mandatory military service.

You will use at least 4 prompts in a conversation, monitoring at each step whether the AI is staying accurate, balanced, and on topic.

Write your school's AI policy

Draft a realistic, principled AI use policy for your class — then defend it to your peers.

▼
The task

Your school probably doesn't have a clear AI policy yet — or if it does, it may not reflect students' real experience of AI tools. You are going to draft one. This is a genuine management task: setting rules about when and how AI should be used, and making sure those rules serve learning rather than undermine it.

  1. Use an AI to help you understand what makes a good AI policy for schools — and what issues need to be addressed.
  2. Draft a policy for your own class, covering: academic work, creative projects, exams, personal wellbeing use, and transparency/attribution.
  3. Then use AI to stress-test your policy — find the weaknesses, loopholes, or missing cases.
  4. Write a final version that you would genuinely be comfortable presenting to your teacher.
💡 Real challenge: can you write a policy that is strict enough to protect learning, but flexible enough to embrace AI as a genuine tool? Most policies get one and miss the other.

Basic

How does it actually work?

Investigate the technical foundations of a specific AI system and explain it in your own words — without the hype.

▼
The task

Most people use AI without understanding anything about how it works. That's a bit like driving a car without knowing that engines need fuel, or that brakes use friction. You don't need to be an engineer — but you do need to understand the basics to make good decisions about when to trust AI.

  1. Choose one AI system you use or have heard of: ChatGPT, Google Translate, Spotify's recommendation engine, image recognition on your phone, or another tool.
  2. Use an AI to explain how it works — in simple terms, step by step.
  3. Then explain it yourself, in your own words, as if you were explaining it to a 12-year-old.
  4. Identify 2 things the system cannot do, and explain why — technically, not just "it's limited".

02
Intermediate

Find the bias in the machine

Design a test to detect potential bias in an AI system, run the experiment, and interpret what you find.

▼
The task

AI bias is not a glitch — it is a feature of how AI systems are built. When training data reflects historical inequalities or the assumptions of the people who built the system, those biases show up in the outputs. Your job is to design and run a test.

  1. Form a hypothesis: what kind of bias do you think might exist in the AI tool you're going to test? (e.g. gender bias, cultural bias, language bias, political bias)
  2. Design 4 to 6 test prompts that probe for this bias. Keep them parallel — only change the variable you're testing.
  3. Run the test and record the outputs side by side.
  4. Analyse: what did you find? Is there evidence of bias?

Example hypotheses:

  • "If I ask for a story about a doctor, AI will default to male pronouns"
  • "AI will describe Norwegian culture more positively than cultures it associates with poverty"
  • "AI will produce longer, more detailed responses to prompts in English than in Norwegian"
  • "AI will suggest more male names than female names when asked for famous scientists"

For Educators

The AILit Framework

This workbook is built on the OECD / European Commission AI Literacy Framework for Primary and Secondary Education (AILit Framework, 2026). The framework defines four domains of AI literacy — Engage, Create, Manage, and Shape — and provides competences, learner expectations, and classroom scenarios for educators to adapt.

Engage with AI

Become a critical and responsible participant in a world marked by AI. Identify AI systems, evaluate outputs, and analyse societal impacts.

Create with AI

Use AI as a creative partner while maintaining human agency. Explore ideas, iterate, and consider originality and intellectual property.

Manage AI

Divide work intentionally between humans and AI. Know when to use it, when not to, and how to maintain oversight throughout.

Shape AI

Improve AI systems to reflect human values. Investigate how systems work, evaluate them critically, and propose improvements for societal benefit.

How this workbook relates to the framework

Each domain in this workbook contains four tasks designed for Norwegian upper secondary students (Vg1 Vg2 Vg3, ages 16 to 18). The tasks progress from basic to advanced and are aligned with the framework's competence statements, learner expectations, and underlying knowledge, skills, and attitudes (KSA) structure. All tasks require students to use real AI tools, reflect critically in writing, and apply metacognitive strategies throughout.

The workbook was designed by Barbara Anna Zielonka as a practical classroom implementation of the AILit Framework. Teachers are encouraged to adapt, extend, or sequence the tasks to suit their subject area, available tools, and student backgrounds. The framework itself is non-binding and explicitly designed for contextual adaptation.
